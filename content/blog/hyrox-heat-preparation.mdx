Most Hyrox events are indoor, but indoor doesn't mean cold. A packed arena with 3,000 athletes warming up, racing, and recovering produces enough heat to push the air temperature to 24-28°C even in February. Summer events in older venues without strong air-handling can be hotter.

This guide is what to do about it.

## What heat does to your race

Above 24°C, the body has to divert blood flow to the skin for cooling. That blood isn't available for the working muscles. Three measurable consequences:

- **Heart rate climbs 5-15 bpm for the same effort.**
- **Sustained aerobic capacity drops 3-8%.**
- **Sweat rate rises by 30-100%, accelerating dehydration.**

For a 90-minute Hyrox at 26°C, expect a 3-5 minute slower finish than the same effort at 18°C. This isn't weakness; it's physiology.

## Heat acclimation: training adaptation

The body adapts to heat. The protocol:

- **2-3 weeks of regular hot-environment training.**
- **10-14 sessions** at moderate intensity, 45-90 minutes each.
- **Add layers** if your training environment isn't naturally hot. A long-sleeved top + jacket can simulate 4-6°C of additional heat.

What changes after acclimation:
- Sweat starts earlier in the session.
- Sweat is more dilute (less salt loss per litre).
- Plasma volume expands 7-12%.
- Perceived effort at the same heart rate drops.

For a race in 8 weeks, start heat training 4-5 weeks out. Acclimation peaks around week 2 and plateaus.

## Race-week hydration

48 hours before race:
- **Increase water intake by 500-1000 ml/day** above baseline.
- **Add electrolytes** to one bottle per day (sodium 500-1000 mg, plus potassium and magnesium).
- **Monitor urine colour.** Light yellow = good. Dark = drink more. Completely clear = over-hydrated, add electrolytes.

24 hours before:
- **Continue hydrated state.**
- **Add salt to meals.** Don't go crazy; an extra 1-2 g sodium per day is plenty.

Race morning:
- **500 ml of water with electrolytes** 2-3 hours before.
- **200-300 ml** 30 minutes before.
- **Final sip** 10 minutes before start.

## Pre-cooling

In the hour before the race, three pre-cooling techniques work:

1. **Cold water on wrists and back of neck.** 60-90 seconds of cold flannel application. Drops core temperature 0.2-0.4°C.
2. **Cold drink.** 300-500 ml of cold (not iced) water in the hour before. Internal cooling.
3. **Stay in the shade.** Avoid baking in the sun in your warm-up gear.

What doesn't work: ice baths (constricts blood flow, can backfire), excessive caffeine (raises core temperature).

## During the race

Sip water at every aid station. Don't gulp; it doesn't absorb faster and risks GI upset.

If the race is genuinely hot (28°C+):
- **Drink at every aid station** (most events have stations after stations 2, 4, 6).
- **Pour water on the head and wrists** if available. Evaporative cooling helps.
- **Pace conservatively** in the first 5 km. Heat compounds across the race; what feels easy at station 2 feels impossible at station 7.

## What to wear in heat

Lighter than your training kit usually:

- **Lightest breathable top** you own. Vest tops work. Avoid cotton.
- **Shorts** preferred over leggings.
- **Sweat-wicking sports bra** for women.
- **Headband** to keep sweat out of eyes (more important in heat).

Skip:
- **Compression garments.** Trap heat. Marginal performance benefit; significant heat cost.
- **Hats.** Sometimes useful outdoors but inside they trap heat against the head.
- **Long sleeves.** Even thin ones add 1-2°C of effective body temperature.

## Pacing adjustments

For a race 3-5°C hotter than your training environment, accept that your time will be 3-5 minutes slower. Pace for the conditions, not your historic PB.

The mental shift: aim for a controlled, well-executed race, not a fast race. The athletes who try to beat their cool-weather PB on a hot day are the ones who blow up at station 6.

## Watch the warning signs

Heat exhaustion symptoms during a race:

- **Stop sweating** despite working hard.
- **Goosebumps** despite heat.
- **Sudden dizziness or confusion.**
- **Muscle cramping** in unusual places.
- **Pale, clammy skin.**

If you experience these, stop. Get to a cool area. Drink water. Find a medic.

A finishing time is not worth heat stroke. Recovery from heat exhaustion takes weeks; recovery from a slow Hyrox takes days.

## Outdoor hot events

Some Hyrox events run partially outdoors (summer, Mediterranean venues). Additional considerations:

- **Sunscreen.** SPF 30+ on exposed skin. Sunburn during a race is a real possibility.
- **Sunglasses.** Useful for the runs.
- **More water than indoor.** Add 200-300 ml to pre-race hydration.

## Training during a hot summer

If you're training through summer for an autumn race:

- **Embrace the heat as acclimation.** Don't only train in the cool early morning.
- **Hydrate aggressively post-session.** Body weight change is the guide: 1.5 L per kg lost.
- **One easy day per week.** Heat training adds cumulative load; you need more recovery than usual.

## The honest summary

Heat is part of Hyrox. Even indoor events get warm. Train for it (heat acclimation 2-3 weeks before), hydrate for it (48 hours of deliberate fuelling), pre-cool for it (cold water + cold drinks), and pace for it (3-5 minutes slower than cool-weather target).

Don't fight the conditions. A controlled race in heat is better than a blown-up race chasing a cool-day PB. The wins come from athletes who respect what the temperature does to physiology.

The thermostat doesn't care about your training. Adjust the strategy and you still finish strong.
