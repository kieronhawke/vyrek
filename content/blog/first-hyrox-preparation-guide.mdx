A first Hyrox is one of the simplest endurance events to enter and one of the easiest to get wrong. Eight running kilometres, broken up by eight functional stations, in the order every athlete in the building runs it. No tactical surprises. Pure execution.

This guide walks through the whole arc, what the race actually is, how to train for it from where you are right now, the kit you need, how to fuel, and the small race-morning decisions that compound. Written for someone signing up for the first time who wants to finish strong, not just survive.

## What Hyrox is, in one paragraph

Hyrox is a standardised fitness race. Everyone runs the same eight 1,000-metre laps, and between each lap completes the same station in the same order, ski erg, sled push, sled pull, burpee broad jumps, rowing, farmers carry, sandbag lunges, wall balls. Same weights for everyone within a category (men's, women's, doubles, pro, masters). That standardisation is the point: your time is comparable globally, against athletes of any nationality, in any city.

If you've raced a half marathon, a triathlon, or a Spartan, you'll find Hyrox familiar but distinct. The running matters; the stations matter more. The athletes who finish in the top half aren't necessarily the fastest runners, they're the ones who lose the least time on the stations.

## The training arc: 8 to 12 weeks

You can finish a Hyrox on six weeks of preparation if you arrive with a baseline of running fitness and gym strength. You'll be more comfortable on 12. Anything beyond 16 weeks is over-engineering for a first race.

Three principles to plan around:

**1. Specificity wins.** The closer your training session looks to a Hyrox station, the more it transfers. A sled push on Monday transfers more to race day than a barbell back squat, even though the squat builds raw strength faster. Don't skip strength work, but bias your week towards moves you'll do under fatigue on race day.

**2. Run more than you think.** Half the race is running. First-timers underestimate this because the stations look intimidating. The brutal truth is that station times are similar for everyone in your category; the time difference between finishers is largely the running. If you can only fit in four sessions a week, two of them should involve running.

**3. Recover.** A 12-week build is long enough that you can't hammer every session. Two hard days, two moderate days, one easy day, two rest days is a structure that survives life. The athletes who burn out at week eight tried to make every session hard.

## A typical training week for a first-timer

Here's what a four-day week looks like in our First Race programme. Adapt the day labels to your schedule.

- **Day 1 (Tuesday):** Hyrox hybrid. 4 × 800 m running with two stations between each set. Race-pace intent. 60 minutes.
- **Day 2 (Thursday):** Strength, hinge, push, pull, core. Heavy enough you'd round at rep 9 of 10. 60 minutes.
- **Day 3 (Saturday):** Long aerobic. 60-75 minutes at conversational pace. Treadmill, road, or trail. Zone 2.
- **Day 4 (Sunday):** Mixed-modal, three rounds of run + station + run + station. Threshold effort. 45 minutes.

Rest days are real rest days. Walks count. Yoga counts. Mobility counts. A second strength session does not count.

## The kit list

You need less than the internet suggests. Stripping it back:

- **Trainers.** A neutral road trainer with a 4-8 mm drop works for almost everyone. If you've got knee or calf issues, a slightly higher drop helps. The Nike Pegasus, the Saucony Endorphin Speed, or the Adidas Adios Pro are the most common Hyrox shoes in the elite field. Don't buy new shoes the week of the race.
- **Shorts and a top you've trained in.** The race is unforgiving on novel kit. Whatever you wore for your hardest training session is your race kit.
- **Gloves, optional.** The sled rope and the wall ball give a few people raw hands. Gym gloves help. Most racers go bare-handed.
- **A timing belt.** Provided by the event. Don't worry about it.

That's it. You don't need compression socks, calf sleeves, or a sled-specific shoe.

## Race-day pacing: the pattern that works

The single biggest mistake first-timers make is going out too fast on the opening 1-km run. The crowd, the music, the adrenaline, everything pulls you into a 4-minute kilometre when you should be running 5:30s.

The pacing pattern that works for most first-timers:

- **First two runs:** 30 seconds slower per kilometre than your steady running pace. This feels almost too easy. It isn't.
- **Stations 1-4:** Steady, not stoppy. The sled push is the hardest station for most people. If you blow your legs here, the next six stations punish you for an hour.
- **Runs 3-5:** Settle into a rhythm that matches your station effort. If you're walking 20 metres after each station, you've gone too hard.
- **Stations 5-8:** This is where the race is won. Wall balls at the end will feel like Everest if you've been racing the first half.
- **Final run + finish:** Whatever you have. You earned the right to empty the tank.

A target time for a healthy first-timer with 10 weeks of preparation: 90 to 105 minutes. Under 90 is excellent. Over 120 is fine, you finished.

## Fuelling the build, then the race

Three things matter for a first-timer:

1. **Eat enough.** A hard Hyrox training week burns 400-600 calories more per day than most people realise. Under-fuelled training is slow, sloppy, and injury-prone. Most first-timers don't need a diet plan; they need to stop eating like someone who isn't training.
2. **Carbs the night before.** A bowl of pasta, rice, or potatoes the evening before tops up muscle glycogen. Skip the heavy sauces.
3. **Race morning.** A familiar carbohydrate breakfast two to three hours before your wave, porridge, white toast and jam, a banana and yoghurt. Low fibre, low fat, moderate volume. Sip water and a small electrolyte mix on the way to the venue.

What we don't recommend: a new gel mid-race that you've never trained with, caffeine you don't normally drink, or anything labelled "pre-workout" containing fifteen ingredients you can't pronounce.

## The week of the race

Day 7: short Hyrox-style session, half the volume of a normal training day. 30 minutes total.
Day 5-6: easy runs only. 30-40 minutes Zone 1-2.
Day 3-4: rest or walk. Mobility is fine.
Day 1-2 (race day eve): 15 minutes of light movement, then dinner and bed. Lay out kit.

The day of the race: wake up two and a half hours before your wave. Eat breakfast. Use the bathroom. Walk to the venue if it's close. Warm up 25 minutes before your start with five minutes of easy jogging, two minutes of dynamic stretches, and one practice sled push at half weight if there's a warm-up area.

## The mental model

Treat the race as eight little races. Don't think about minute 90 when you're at minute 12. Think about getting to the next station with your breathing under control. Stations are simple problems: get to the end of the lane, in form, without stopping. Runs are simple problems: get to the next station feeling like you can do the station.

Most people, when they finish their first Hyrox, surprise themselves. The pattern goes: nervous walk to the start, conservative first kilometre, gradual realisation that they can do this, controlled middle stations, a hard burn at wall balls, a final 1 km that feels longer than the previous seven, and a finish line that's much closer than they expected.

Sign up. Train consistently. Pace. The finish line is closer than you think.
