<Callout tone="info" title="The sneaky station">
The farmers carry sits at station 7 of 8. By this point you've done five other stations and 6 km of running. The carry doesn't ask for much absolute strength, but it punishes any weakness in your grip endurance, your shoulder positioning, or your aerobic recovery. Get those right and the carry is where you recover for the wall balls.
</Callout>

The farmers carry is the station that exposes the difference between strong and conditioned. A strong athlete can pick up 48 kg of kettlebell weight and walk with it. A conditioned athlete can pick it up, walk 200 metres without dropping it, and arrive at the wall balls with a heart rate that's actually lower than when they started. That's the gap. And it's bridgeable in 12 weeks of training.

This piece breaks down the grip strategy, the walking technique, the breathing pattern, and the most common race-day mistake that turns a 90-second carry into a 3-minute disaster.

## What the farmers carry actually demands

Two kettlebells, hanging at your sides. 200 metres of forward walking, broken into four 50-metre laps (in most venues, some larger race floors run two 100-metre laps). Total time for age-group athletes: 90 seconds to 3 minutes. Total time for elite athletes: 75-90 seconds.

The demands stack like this:

1. **Grip endurance.** Not maximum grip strength, the kettlebells aren't heavy enough for that. What kills people is the sustained, isometric grip at sub-maximal load.
2. **Shoulder packing.** Letting the shoulders pull forward and down (which they want to under load) loads the upper traps and degrades your walking pattern.
3. **Aerobic recovery.** The carry is meant to be a relative recovery station. If your heart rate is climbing rather than steady or dropping during the carry, your pacing on the previous stations was too aggressive.

## Grip strategy: full grip, hands deep

Three rules:

**1. Get your hand deep on the handle.** Hook the handle into the heel of your palm, not the fingertips. The further from the joint your fingers, the more leverage they have. Athletes who grip with their fingertips fail at metre 90.

**2. Use a full grip, not a hook grip.** The hook grip (thumb wrapped) saves a fraction of a second on the pick-up but the thumb fatigues fast under sustained load. Across 90 seconds of carrying, a full grip wins.

**3. Don't death-grip.** Squeezing the handle at 100% from the start is a guaranteed grip failure by metre 120. Use the lightest grip that holds the kettlebell. Re-grip every 25 metres if your hands relax during the walk.

If you've never trained grip endurance specifically, expect your forearms to feel like wood by metre 150 of your first farmers carry under fatigue. That's not a strength problem, it's an endurance problem, and it disappears within four weeks of dedicated training.

## Walking technique: short steps, tall posture

Four cues:

**1. Stand tall.** Pull your shoulders down and back. Pretend a string runs from the top of your head to the ceiling. The natural reflex under load is to hunch, fight it.

**2. Short, fast steps.** Stride length wants to be about 70% of your normal walking stride. Long strides under load destabilise the carry and waste vertical energy on bobbing. Short strides keep the kettlebells in a tight oscillation.

**3. Look 5 metres ahead.** Not at the floor (rounds the spine), not at the finish (mentally taxing). Eyes at horizon, neck neutral.

**4. Breathe through pursed lips.** The carry should be brisk walking pace, not a run. Aim for a 4:2 breathing pattern, four steps in, two steps out, to drop heart rate over the course of the carry.

## The pick-up and the drop

The pick-up is two seconds that decide the next 90:

1. Stand between the kettlebells, feet shoulder-width.
2. Hip-hinge straight down, bend at the hips first, then knees.
3. Grip the handles deep. Squeeze the bells into the floor as you stand.
4. Stand tall in one smooth movement. The lift should feel light if you've set up correctly.

If you have to wiggle, re-grip, or shuffle the bells before you start walking, your set-up was wrong. Practise the pick-up in training until it's one movement.

The set-down at the end:

1. Hip-hinge down. Don't drop the bells from chest height, too jarring, looks scrappy, can damage your wrists if the kettlebells torque.
2. Bells touch the floor, you stand up, you transition.

## Pacing strategy: even, never accelerating

The number-one race-day mistake on the farmers carry is starting fast and slowing. Athletes feel strong off the running into the station, pick up the bells, and walk the first 50 metres at a brisk shuffle. The pace gets ahead of the breathing. By metre 100 they're breathing hard but tired, and the grip is fading.

What works:

- **Lap 1 (0-50 m): brisk walk, not jog.** About 80% of your maximum sustainable carry pace.
- **Lap 2 (50-100 m): same pace.** Breathing normalising.
- **Lap 3 (100-150 m): same pace.** This is where everyone wants to slow. Don't.
- **Lap 4 (150-200 m): same pace.** Heart rate should be dropping as you arrive at the wall balls.

If you're going to drop the kettlebells once, the right place is at the end of lap 2 (100 m mark). One five-second reset there is cleaner than three desperate ten-second stops in the second half.

## Drills that transfer to race day

Three drills, twice a week during the build phase:

**1. Heavy farmers walks (90-100% of race weight).** 4 sets × 30-40 metres. Builds absolute carry capacity. Done at the end of strength sessions, fresh enough to maintain posture.

**2. Race-weight under fatigue.** After a 5-minute run interval, immediately pick up race-weight kettlebells and walk 100 metres. 3 sets. This is the actual race scenario, fatigued aerobic system, sustained grip. Most race-day failures happen because athletes never train this transition.

**3. Grip-only finishers.** Dead hangs from a pull-up bar for time, after sessions. 60 seconds × 3 sets builds the isometric grip endurance specifically. Cheap, fast, effective.

## What good looks like

A good Hyrox farmers carry at age-group level:

- Time: 100-130 seconds.
- Drops: zero or one (planned).
- Stride: short, fast, even.
- Posture: tall throughout, shoulders back, neck neutral.
- Heart rate: stable or dropping across the 200 metres.
- Exit breathing: controlled, ready to wall ball.

If your carry is taking 3 minutes with multiple unplanned drops, the fix isn't more carries in training. The fix is grip endurance (dead hangs, longer carries at lower weight) and pacing discipline on the kilometres before.

<Callout tone="tip" title="Pace">
The single best farmers carry indicator is your heart rate during the walk. If your watch shows your heart rate climbing during the carry, you went too hard on the run before. If it's stable or dropping, you paced it right. Race-rehearse this and you'll know what "right" feels like before race day.
</Callout>

## How Vyrek programmes the carry

The First Race programme builds carry capacity from week 1 with shorter, lighter carries that progress to race-weight by week 8. Sub-90 and Pro programmes layer in race-weight carries under fatigue, usually after a high-intensity interval, twice a week from week 4. Grip endurance work (dead hangs, dumbbell hangs, towel pull-ups) runs as a finisher throughout.

Doubles programming splits the carry between athletes, each does 100 metres of the 200, but the demands per athlete are higher because the second athlete picks up immediately, with no run recovery. The doubles programme accounts for that with paired interval work.

## In practice

The farmers carry is a deception. It looks like a strength station. It's actually an aerobic recovery station for athletes who paced well, and a death-march for athletes who didn't. Grip endurance, short strides, even pacing, and honest kilometres before the carry, those four things turn it into a 90-second walk that resets you for the wall balls. Skip any one and the carry will quietly cost you minutes you won't get back.
