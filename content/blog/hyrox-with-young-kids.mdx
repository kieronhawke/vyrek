Most Hyrox training plans assume the athlete has 90 minutes a day, six days a week, and a flexible schedule. Most parents don't. This is the plan that works when your training has to fit between school runs, nursery pickups, and bath time.

## The realistic time budget

Be honest about what you have:

- **4 sessions per week, 60 minutes each = 4 hours.**
- **Plus 30 min of mobility / commute time = 5 hours total.**
- **Plus mental space for race preparation = 6 hours commitment.**

If your week looks busier than that, the answer is fewer sessions at higher intensity, not the same sessions less recovered.

## The 4-day plan

This is the plan that works for most parent athletes:

| Day | Session | Time |
|---|---|---|
| Tue early am | Aerobic intervals: 5 × 1 km at race pace + 90s rest | 50 min |
| Thu early am | Strength: deadlift, single-leg, push, pull | 60 min |
| Sat morning | Race simulation OR long easy aerobic (alternate) | 60-75 min |
| Sun morning | Easy aerobic 45-60 min | 45 min |

Three sessions are at sacred times (early morning or weekend morning when kids may still be asleep). One is at a flexible time.

If the Sunday session is impossible, drop it. The other three are what get you race-ready.

## Early morning protocol

The 5:30-6:30 am session has rules:

1. **Pack the kit the night before.** Including water, snack, and the workout written down.
2. **Don't shower before; shower after.** Saves time.
3. **Eat lightly: banana + coffee.** Real food after.
4. **Be back home by school-run time.**
5. **Don't expect to feel great in the first 10 minutes.** Warm-up takes longer at 5:30 am.

The athletes who maintain this for 12 weeks are the ones whose families know the rhythm. Family buy-in matters.

## When the day blows up

Every parent training week has at least one day where everything goes wrong. Kid sick, work crisis, partner needs the morning. The rule:

**Don't try to make up the missed session.** Move on to the next planned session. Stacking two hard sessions on consecutive days when you're already short on sleep is the fastest way to get hurt.

If you have to drop a session, drop in this order:
1. **Sunday easy aerobic** (drop first; recoverable).
2. **Thursday strength** (drop if necessary; harder to maintain progression).
3. **Saturday simulation / long aerobic** (drop only if completely impossible).
4. **Tuesday intervals** (try not to drop; the highest-payoff session).

## Buggy running

Running with a jogging buggy:

- **Easy aerobic sessions: yes.** Adds 10-15% to perceived effort but maintains the aerobic base.
- **Race-pace work: avoid.** The wheel friction makes pace targets meaningless.
- **Long aerobic: yes if route is flat.** Uphills become workouts.

A good jogging buggy makes weekend runs possible without childcare. Worth the investment.

## Training at home with kids around

When you can't escape, two protocols work:

**Home strength session (45 min):**
- 5 × 5 trap-bar deadlift (if you have a barbell)
- 4 × 8 single-leg deadlift (kettlebell)
- 4 × 10 push-up (or weighted)
- 4 × 8 pull-up (or assisted)
- 4 × 12 KB swing

Done in your garage or spare room. Kids can pad around while you lift. Don't try to focus deeply; you won't. Just do the work.

**Home aerobic intervals (40 min):**
- 8 × 3 minutes hard / 1 minute easy on a stationary bike or rower
- Or 8 × 400 m hard / 200 m easy on a treadmill

Loud headphones not an option around small kids. Use the time to half-watch a show on a tablet while training.

## What to skip entirely

For parent athletes, some traditional Hyrox training elements aren't worth the time:

- **Two-a-days.** No.
- **90-minute strength sessions.** Cap at 60.
- **Long-easy-runs over 90 minutes.** Diminishing returns for parent recovery.
- **Specialty mobility / massage / cold plunge protocols.** Sleep is more impactful than any of these.

## Sleep matters more than the extra session

The single biggest parent-athlete lever: protect sleep. 7 hours of sleep with 4 quality sessions beats 5 hours of sleep with 6 sessions. Always.

This means:
- **No 4:30 am workouts** if you're going to bed at 11.
- **Skip the late-night training** that ruins sleep.
- **Reduce caffeine after 2 pm.**
- **Phone out of the bedroom.**

## The race-day reality

A parent training 4 hours a week for 12 weeks can finish a Hyrox in:

- **Under 110 minutes** as a first-timer (any fitness background).
- **Sub-100 minutes** with a solid base going in.
- **Sub-90 minutes** with prior Hyrox experience and a cooperative schedule.

You're not winning the elite category. You're finishing a hard race, fit, and back home for Sunday afternoon family time.

## The mental cost is real

Training while parenting young kids is mentally heavy. Three things help:

1. **Make it a habit, not a decision.** Tuesday 5:30 am is non-negotiable. Don't decide each morning whether to go.
2. **Tell your family why you're doing it.** Race date, finishing goal, what it means. Family support is the foundation.
3. **Celebrate the small wins.** A consistent training week is an accomplishment when you have young kids.

## What changes when kids get older

When your youngest hits 5-6 and life calms down slightly:

- **Add a 5th session.** Probably mid-week aerobic.
- **Lengthen the Saturday long run** to 90 minutes if you want.
- **Add 15 minutes of mobility 2x per week.**

By the time your youngest is 8+, you can train like a regular adult athlete. Until then, the 4-day plan is the sustainable version.

## The honest summary

You don't need 10 hours a week to train for Hyrox. You need 4-5 focused hours, the discipline to protect early-morning sessions, and the wisdom to drop the easy session before the hard one.

Most parents who race Hyrox finish in 85-105 minutes. That's a strong race for any human. It's an extraordinary race for someone juggling small kids.

Pick the race. Tell your partner. Train consistently. The race date will arrive faster than you think.
