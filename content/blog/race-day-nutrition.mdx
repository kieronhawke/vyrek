Race-day nutrition is one of those topics where forum advice grows wilder by the day. Carb-loading protocols, expensive electrolyte cocktails, specific gel timings down to the kilometre. Most of it is irrelevant for a 90-minute race.

Here's what actually matters: arriving with a full tank of glycogen, fuelled but not bloated, hydrated but not soggy. The plan below works for most first-time Hyrox racers. Adjust the volumes to your body weight and what you've trained with — never try anything new on race day.

## The 48 hours before

The simplest carb-loading approach for a 90-minute race:

- **Two days before:** Slightly increase carbohydrate share of meals. If you normally eat a chicken-and-greens dinner, add a portion of rice or pasta. Don't double your overall food intake — that creates digestive distress, not race-day fuel.
- **Day before:** Normal meals, with the dinner being a carbohydrate-led plate. Pasta with a light tomato or olive oil sauce. Sushi. Rice and grilled fish. Skip heavy dairy, heavy fats, and high-fibre vegetables (broccoli, beans) — they slow digestion and tend to show up at the worst moment.
- **Day-before hydration:** Sip water through the day. Don't chug a litre at 9 pm — you'll wake up at 3 am.
- **Sleep:** The most underrated piece of race prep. Aim for 8 hours two nights before, in case race-eve nerves cut into the eve-of-race sleep.

## Race morning: 3 hours, 2 hours, 1 hour

Working backwards from your wave start time:

**3 hours out — Wake up. Drink 300-500 ml water with a small electrolyte mix.** Most pre-mixed electrolyte sachets work. Skip the "energy" versions with stimulants.

**2.5 hours out — Breakfast.**

The Hyrox breakfast template:

- A carbohydrate-led main: porridge with banana and honey; white toast with jam; rice cakes with peanut butter (small portion); a bagel with honey.
- A modest protein top-up: a poached egg or a small Greek yoghurt.
- Low fibre, low fat. Save the seeded toast and bowls of berries for any other day.
- 400-600 calories total. Modest, not a feast.

What works for most people:
- Porridge (50 g oats) cooked with water + 1 banana + 1 tbsp honey. ~400 cal.
- 2 slices white toast + jam + 1 boiled egg. ~450 cal.
- 1 plain bagel + honey + small yoghurt. ~500 cal.

What doesn't work for most people: a "healthy" breakfast bowl with chia seeds, granola, blueberries, and yoghurt. Fibre overload pre-race is unkind to your race day.

**1.5 hours out — Last big sip of water and a coffee if you train with coffee.** A normal coffee or tea is fine. Don't take a triple espresso if you usually have one cup.

**45 minutes out — Small carb top-up.** A banana, half an energy bar, or a few dates. Optional but helpful if you ate breakfast on the early side. Sip water.

**20 minutes out — 200 ml water with electrolytes.** Stop drinking after this. You don't want to be desperate for the bathroom on the second run.

**Race start.**

## During the race

Most first-timers don't need to eat during a 90-minute race. The tank you arrived with is enough. Two situations where mid-race fuel helps:

- **Race over 100 minutes.** Add one gel or 30 g of carbs in chewable form at minute 45-60.
- **Heavy sweater or hot venue.** A second electrolyte sip at the run-to-station water station around the halfway point.

Things to avoid mid-race:

- Solid food. Your gut isn't in the mood.
- Caffeine pills or strong stimulants. Heart-rate spike during sled push is unwelcome.
- Salt tablets unless you've used them in training and know you tolerate them.

Water at the water stations is fine. Sip, don't chug.

## The first 60 minutes after the finish

This is the highest-leverage nutrition window of the race day. Your muscles are most receptive to carbohydrate and protein replenishment within the hour after you finish.

Two windows, two priorities:

**0-15 minutes — Rehydrate.** Sip water with electrolytes. 500 ml over the first 15 minutes. Don't smash a litre immediately or your stomach will revolt.

**15-60 minutes — Refuel.**

- 50-80 g carbohydrate.
- 20-30 g protein.
- Something you can keep down even while your heart rate is still elevated.

Practical options:
- A protein shake + a banana + a flapjack.
- A chicken and rice bowl from the venue food stall.
- A Greek yoghurt + honey + a bagel.

What we don't recommend: a celebration pint immediately. Alcohol is fine after a proper recovery meal, but a pint on an empty, dehydrated stomach 20 minutes after a Hyrox is a fast way to feel terrible by the evening.

## Hours 1-24 post-race

Eat normally, lean towards carbohydrate, prioritise sleep. The micro-tears in your muscles from the race repair fastest with good nutrition and 8-9 hours of sleep.

Common post-race meal templates:
- Burger and chips (sometimes the best thing is the obvious thing).
- A pasta or risotto with a small protein.
- A roast dinner if you raced on a Saturday.

Don't push a strict diet 24 hours after a race. The body has done a hard thing; it deserves a generous refeed.

## The supplements question

The honest answer: most first-timers don't need any supplements they aren't already using.

The list of things shown to help, in rough order of evidence:
1. **Caffeine** (if you tolerate it and use it in training). 1-3 mg per kg body weight, taken 45-60 minutes pre-race.
2. **Carbohydrate** (food works, gels work, dextrose powders work — pick what your gut handles).
3. **Electrolytes** (especially in hot venues or for heavy sweaters).
4. **Beetroot juice** (modest evidence for endurance; taste isn't for everyone).

Everything below this on the supplement aisle has less evidence than the marketing implies.

## The pattern most first-timers settle on

After a season of racing, most Hyrox athletes converge on a similar pattern:

- Carbohydrate dinner the night before.
- 400-500 cal carbohydrate-led breakfast 2.5 hours out.
- Coffee 90 minutes out (if a coffee drinker).
- Banana or dates 45 minutes out.
- Electrolyte water in the 20 minutes before start.
- Water station sips during the race.
- Protein and carbs within an hour of finishing.

No mystery. No specialist products. The race rewards consistency in training and a familiar fuelling plan on race day — not novelty. Eat what you've trained with. Drink what your stomach knows. Cross the line, eat properly, and sleep.
