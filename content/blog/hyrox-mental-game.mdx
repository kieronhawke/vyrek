<Callout tone="info" title="The race is mostly mental from station 4 onward">
By the burpee broad jump (station 4), you've done 3 km of running plus two sled stations. Your fitness is what it is. From this point forward, the difference between racers of equal fitness is mental: pacing discipline, breathing control, and the willingness to maintain rhythm when everything tells you to back off.
</Callout>

The Hyrox racer who wins is the one who's already won the race in their head before the gun goes.

That sounds like a quote from a motivational poster. It's not. It's the literal mechanism: athletes who arrive at the start line with a written pacing plan, a three-word mental cue, and a rehearsed pain framework consistently outperform athletes of similar fitness who don't.

This piece breaks down the three frameworks. None of them are mystical. All of them are trainable. None require any natural mental advantage you don't already have.

## Framework 1. The pacing rule (hold back twice)

The single most damaging mental moment of a Hyrox race is the first kilometre. The adrenaline is high, the legs feel fresh, the crowd is loud, and you're naturally going to push 20-30 seconds per kilometre faster than your sustainable pace.

You don't notice this happening because the feedback (lactic acid, breathing rate) lags by 90 seconds. By the time your body tells you you went too hard, you're 1.5 km in and your sled push is already going to be slow.

**The rule: hold back twice.**

- **First check at 500m.** Are you running at the goal pace you wrote down before the race? If yes, hold. If you're 10+ seconds faster, slow down right now. It feels embarrassing, runners behind you might pass, let them.
- **Second check at 1 km exactly.** Run your first km 10-15 seconds *slower* than your target average. If the goal average is 4:30, run km 1 at 4:40-4:45.

Why slower than average: the back half always slows. Even the elite racers positive-split, but they positive-split by 5-10 seconds per km, not 45. By giving yourself 10-15 extra seconds in km 1, you bank the cushion needed for km 6-8.

If you do nothing else mentally in the race, do this. The "hold back twice" rule alone is worth 2-4 minutes off most age-group athletes' finish times.

## Framework 2. Three-word cues (yours, not borrowed)

When the pain hits around station 4-5, your prefrontal cortex starts shutting down. Complex thoughts disappear. What remains is short, repeatable phrases.

Pick three words before the race. Say them on the start line. Repeat them when the pain hits.

Mine are: **patience, posture, breath.**

- *Patience*, for the first kilometre, to not chase the runners ahead.
- *Posture*, for the sled push, to stay low and locked.
- *Breath*, for the wall balls, to keep the three-count rhythm.

That's it. Three words. Borrowed from anyone? No, pick yours.

A few rules:

1. **Pick three, no more.** Four is too many to remember at threshold. Three is the limit.
2. **Pick what's true for your patterns.** If your downfall is going out too fast, "patience" is in there. If you collapse on the burpees, "rhythm" is in there.
3. **Write them on your hand.** Sharpie, race morning. Glance at them between stations.
4. **Say them out loud on the start line.** Sound makes them real.

## Framework 3. The pain is the data

Most amateur racers interpret pain as a verdict ("I'm failing, I need to slow down"). Elite racers interpret pain as data ("the system is working, this is the expected sensation at this effort").

The reframe is simple but takes practice. When the pain hits at station 5, instead of:

> "This is awful, I need to stop, I'm going to blow up."

Try:

> "This is the pain I should be in at this effort. The body is producing the right signal. Maintain rhythm."

Why this matters: the first interpretation triggers a pacing change (you slow down to "make the pain stop"). The second interpretation maintains pace. Over a 90-minute race, that maintained pace adds up to minutes.

To make this reframe automatic, practise it in training. Every Hyrox-pattern session, when you hit the wall balls or sandbag lunge in rep 40, deliberately say "the pain is the data" to yourself. Three weeks of this in training and it becomes automatic on race day.

## The burpee broad jump specifically

The burpee broad jump (station 4) is where mental races are won or lost. By rep 10, you're in heart-rate territory you usually only see for 30 seconds. By rep 20, the breath is ragged. Most athletes spiral here.

The rescue protocol:

1. **Three-count breathing.** Breath out on the descent, in on the stand-up, out on the jump. Audible exhale. Loud enough that you can hear it over your own thoughts.
2. **Tight midline.** Eyes 30 cm ahead of your hands. Core braced. Body lands as one unit.
3. **Don't count down, count up.** "I've done 12, I've done 13" beats "I have 14 to go". Adding wins to a tally beats subtracting from a debt.

If you've prepped the burpee broad jump mentally before the race, station 5 onward feels comparatively easy. Most athletes who survive the burpees psychologically finish strong.

## What NOT to do mentally

Three mental anti-patterns that wreck races:

**1. Comparison.** Looking at the runner ahead and trying to match their pace. They might be a sub-60 racer running their easy pace. You'll cook trying to keep up.

**2. Math.** Calculating finish times mid-race. "If I keep this pace I'll finish at X." This is mentally taxing and the numbers are usually wrong. Race the kilometre you're in.

**3. The negotiation.** "I can slow down on the next run and make it back on wall balls." You never make it back. The negotiation is your brain looking for an exit. Maintain pace.

## How to train the mental game

You don't train the mental game by reading books. You train it by deliberately practising pain tolerance under controlled stress:

- **Weekly Hyrox-pattern session** with the three-word cue rehearsal.
- **One race-simulation per month** during peak training (weeks 7-10). Full kit, race-pace, three-word cues said out loud.
- **Visualization** before bed twice a week from week 8 onward. 60 seconds of visualising the first three minutes of the race, where you'll position yourself, how you'll breathe, what you'll say to yourself.

This is not woo. It's neural pattern rehearsal. By race day the pattern is automatic.

## To sum up

You can't out-train the mental game on race day. Pacing discipline is decided in the first 500 metres. Pain interpretation is set in the months of training before. Three-word cues are chosen in advance.

The athletes who race best are the ones whose mental work is finished before the gun goes. The race itself is just the delivery.
