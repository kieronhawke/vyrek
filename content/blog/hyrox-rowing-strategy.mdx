The rower sits at station 5 of 8 in Hyrox. Your race is half done. You're tired but not broken. The next 1,000 m is a chance to settle, breathe, and set up a strong back half.

## What the row actually demands

1,000 m on a Concept2 rower. Standard erg, monitored split per 500 m. You strap in, row, hit the distance, get out. Total time: 3:30-4:30 for most open athletes.

The movement is leg-first: drive with the legs, hinge the hips back, then pull with the arms. The reverse for the recovery: arms forward, hips forward, then legs bend back to the catch. Sequenced wrong, you waste 40% of the power.

## Pace targets by category

| Category | 500 m split | 1000 m time |
|---|---|---|
| Open men (target sub-90) | 2:00-2:05 | 4:00-4:10 |
| Open men (target sub-75) | 1:50-1:55 | 3:40-3:50 |
| Open women (target sub-110) | 2:15-2:25 | 4:30-4:50 |
| Open women (target sub-90) | 2:00-2:10 | 4:00-4:20 |
| Pro / elite | 1:45-1:55 | 3:30-3:50 |

These are race-day targets at station 5 with 35-50 minutes of fatigue in your legs.

## Posture under fatigue

The rowing technique most athletes know is the fresh technique. By station 5, you're tired and a tired athlete rounds the back, hunches the shoulders, and pulls with the arms.

What works under fatigue:

- **Sit tall.** Chest up, shoulders back. Even tired, lift the rib cage. This protects the lower back and lets you breathe.
- **Legs first, always.** The drive starts with the legs. If you start with arms, the legs follow weakly and you lose 30-50 watts.
- **Long recovery.** The slide back to the catch is your rest. Don't rush. Aim for a 1:2 drive-to-recovery ratio (the recovery takes twice as long as the drive).

## Stroke rate

For Hyrox, stroke rate 26-30 per minute is the sweet spot. Higher rate burns more energy for the same boat speed; lower rate means you have to pull harder per stroke.

If you're new to rowing: row at 26 strokes per minute and focus on power per stroke. If you're experienced: 28-30 strokes per minute is your comfortable working zone.

## Breathing pattern

Same as ski erg: one breath per stroke is the cleanest. Inhale on the recovery, exhale on the drive. Sustainable. Predictable.

If you're under-paced and breathing too hard, drop the stroke rate by two and add a beat of inhale on the recovery. Recover within the stroke instead of trying to recover by stopping.

## What kills row times

In order:

1. **Pulling with arms only.** Wastes 40% of the power. The most common technique error.
2. **Bending the wrists.** Keep wrists flat through the pull. Bent wrists lose force transfer.
3. **Rushing the recovery.** Drive at 100%, recovery at 50%. Trying to "speed up" by rushing back is a net negative.
4. **Wrong split target.** Going out at 1:50 for 200 m, finishing at 2:15 because the legs died. Pick one split, hold it for 1000 m.

## The zero-cost transition

The rower-to-run transition is where 5-15 seconds is left on the table by 80% of the field. The technique:

1. **Last stroke at the prescribed split, no sprint finish.** Don't blow up at metre 990.
2. **Sit forward, hands off the handle.** Reach down.
3. **One smooth motion to release foot straps.** Both feet free.
4. **Stand straight.** Take one full breath.
5. **Walk 5-7 steps out of the rower zone.** Lets your head settle and your legs find the floor again.
6. **Break into a jog.** Slow first 30 m, normal pace after.

The athletes who sprint immediately out of the rower lose 5-15 seconds in the first 100 m of the run because their legs are still in rowing pattern and their balance is off.

## Training the row for Hyrox

If your gym has a rower:

- **Session A:** 4 × 500 m at goal split + 90 s rest. Builds the pace.
- **Session B:** 2000 m at goal split + 5 min rest, then 1000 m at goal split. Builds the duration + race simulation.

Weekly, in the 6 weeks before race day. Once if you're well-trained; twice if rowing is a weakness.

If your gym has no rower: ski erg substitutes well; bike erg is acceptable. The aerobic adaptation transfers.

## The honest summary

The row is the most forgiving Hyrox station because the pace target is clear and the technique is well-documented. The athletes who row well are the ones who:

- Pick a 500 m split target and hold it
- Drive with legs, finish with arms
- Sit tall under fatigue
- Recover patiently within each stroke
- Execute the clean transition

Train it twice a week for six weeks. Race it at your target split. Walk-jog out. The post-row 1 km run is where you settle for the back half of the race.
