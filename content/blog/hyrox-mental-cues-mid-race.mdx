<Callout tone="info" title="The mid-race truth">
At station 6, your fitness has done its job. The next 25 minutes are decided by your mind. Athletes who finish strong have rehearsed the script.
</Callout>

The middle of a Hyrox race is where everything hurts and nothing has any clear end in sight. Stations 6, 7, and 8 (farmer's carry, sandbag lunges, wall ball) plus the runs between, plus the final 1 km. About 25-35 minutes of work when you're already 60 minutes deep.

This is where the mental tools earn their keep. Here are eight phrases that work, and the moments to use them.

## 1. "One segment at a time" — when you start thinking about the finish

The moment: somewhere on the run after station 5. The brain starts calculating: "I have three more stations and the final run. That's 25 more minutes. I can't."

The fix: stop counting. The brain handles "this 200 metres" much better than "the next 25 minutes". Race in 200-metre chunks.

The phrase to say internally: "**One segment at a time.**"

## 2. "Hands on the bag" — at the start of farmer's carry

The moment: you arrive at the farmer's carry station, your forearms already feel pre-fatigued from the rower.

The fix: focus on the first physical action. Not the 200 metres ahead. Not the wall ball coming. Just the next motion.

The phrase: "**Hands on the bag. Walk.**"

The cue narrows the focus to the immediate next thing. It works for any station.

## 3. "Steady cadence" — mid-sandbag-lunge

The moment: metre 50 of the lunge. The legs are screaming. The temptation is to speed up to get it over with, or to stop and rest.

The fix: hold the cadence. One lunge every 2-3 seconds. The cadence is the rhythm; the rhythm is the survival mechanism.

The phrase: "**Steady cadence. Step. Touch. Drive.**"

Three words, the lunge motion. Repeat for 50 reps.

## 4. "Two minutes more" — anywhere you want to quit

The moment: most likely the middle of a sandbag lunge or the post-lunge run. The mind says quit.

The fix: commit to two more minutes. Just two. Anyone can do two minutes. After two minutes, decide again. Almost always, the decision is to continue.

The phrase: "**Two minutes more.**"

This works because the brain's quitting decision is usually based on projecting 20-30 minutes of suffering. Reduce the projection to two minutes and the decision changes.

## 5. "First ten, find rhythm" — at the start of wall ball

The moment: arriving at the wall ball station. You're tired. You see 100 (or 75) wall balls ahead.

The fix: don't think about 100. Think about the first 10. Establish rhythm. Then the next 10.

The phrase: "**First ten, find rhythm. Then ten more.**"

Most wall ball strategies are some variation of breaking the set into 10s or 15s. The mental cue matches the physical break-down.

## 6. "Breathe out, throw" — every wall ball rep

The moment: every wall ball rep. The shoulders are gone. The squat is harder. The ball feels heavier.

The fix: a single-rep cue. Inhale on the squat. Exhale on the throw. The exhale is the work.

The phrase: "**Breathe out, throw.**"

Three words per rep. Across 75 or 100 reps, the rhythm becomes hypnotic. Athletes who use this cue rarely have to put the ball down.

## 7. "Finish line in sight" — start of final 1 km

The moment: leaving the wall ball station onto the final 1 km. Everything hurts. The finish line is somewhere ahead.

The fix: reframe the run. You're not running another tired 1 km. You're running to the finish. Different feeling.

The phrase: "**Finish line in sight. Run to it.**"

If you have anything left, this is the place to spend it. Negative split the run if you can.

## 8. "Crossed it" — the last 50 metres

The moment: the finish chute. You can see the line. The crowd is cheering. The body is empty.

The fix: pretend you've already crossed. The last 50 metres are downhill, mental-energy-wise. Open the stride. Let go.

The phrase: "**Crossed it.**"

## When the cues stop working

Sometimes the script doesn't reach you. The pain is too loud or the discouragement too deep. Three things help when the cues alone aren't enough:

**1. Look at another athlete.** Pick someone moving forward. Match them. The shared effort is grounding.

**2. Look at the clock.** Sometimes a number helps. "I've done 65 minutes. The race is 90. I can do 25 more."

**3. Look at the floor.** Reduce field of view. Foot, foot, foot. Don't look at the wall ball pile or the finish line. Just the next foot strike.

## The phrases you don't want

A few internal scripts to avoid:

- **"I can't."** Once said, it's hard to un-say. Replace with "this is hard but I'm doing it".
- **"Why did I sign up?"** Race-day is not the time. Park the question for after.
- **"Just get it over with."** Sets up rushing, which causes blowing up. Replace with "steady cadence, finish this segment".

## Training the script

The cues only work if they're rehearsed. Two ways:

**1. Use them in every race simulation in training.** Speak them out loud. They feel ridiculous in training; they feel essential in racing.

**2. Use them on long runs.** Not because you need them on the run, but because you're building the cue-and-response habit.

After 6-8 weeks of using these cues in training, they fire automatically on race day.

## The honest summary

The mid-race middle is where the brain decides if the body finishes. Eight cues, each for a specific moment, each tested and refined.

The cues feel small. The effect is large. Athletes who use a mental script finish 3-8 minutes faster than athletes of identical fitness who don't. Not because the script makes them stronger; because the script keeps them from quitting early.

Pick the three that resonate with you. Rehearse them in training. Race with them. The middle is survivable; the end is closer than it feels.
