<Callout tone="info" title="The hidden truth">
The sandbag lunge isn't a strength test. It's a posture and pacing test. The athletes who blow up here are the ones who attacked it. The athletes who finish fastest are the ones who held a steady cadence.
</Callout>

Sandbag lunges sit at station 7 of 8 in Hyrox. Your legs are already tired. You've just finished the farmer's carry. You have wall balls and the final 1 km run ahead. The lunge is where many first-timers' races fall apart.

## What the sandbag lunges demand

100 m of walking lunges with a sandbag across your shoulders. The lunge has to be controlled (back knee touching the ground each rep) and the bag has to stay above the shoulders. Drop the bag, do a 5-burpee penalty. Fail to touch the back knee, the rep doesn't count.

The physical demand: posterior chain, quads, hip flexors, core. The aerobic demand: massive. Lunging while breathing under load is one of the hardest combined demands in fitness racing.

## Carry position: shoulders, not anywhere else

Three carry positions athletes use. Only one of them works at race-day distance:

**1. Behind the neck, on the trap shelf.** This is the right answer. Bag balanced across the back of the shoulders, hands gripping the front of the bag near the ears. The weight sits on bone (the shoulders) instead of muscle. You can carry it for the full 100 m without shoulder fatigue.

**2. Bear hug / front rack.** Bag clutched to the chest. Faster to set up but kills the shoulders by metre 30. Avoid.

**3. Overhead.** Looks elite. Wastes massive energy and risks bag drops. Avoid.

The behind-the-neck position takes 5-10 seconds longer to set up before you start lunging. That investment is recovered within the first 20 metres.

## Lunge depth and rhythm

The rep:
- Step forward, controlled.
- Back knee gently touches the floor.
- Drive up through the front heel.
- Front leg fully extends before you start the next rep.
- Alternate legs.

The cadence: one lunge every 2-3 seconds. That's slower than your training body wants to go. Resist the urge to rush.

Common mistake: bouncing the back knee off the floor. That's an injury risk and an automatic no-rep at most events. Touch and stand.

## Breathing: one breath per lunge

The breathing pattern that works for most athletes:

- **Inhale on the step forward.**
- **Brace through the descent.**
- **Exhale through the drive up.**

Repeat. One breath cycle per lunge. Predictable, calming, sustainable for the full 100 m.

The pattern that doesn't work: holding your breath through 3-4 lunges then gasping. You'll go dizzy by metre 50 and either drop the bag or stop.

## The 4-step recovery trick

If you must stop mid-lunge to recover, here's the technique that costs least:

1. **Stand fully upright with the bag still on your shoulders.** Don't drop it.
2. **Take 3-4 slow, controlled deep breaths.** Belly breathing.
3. **Shake out the front leg.** Wiggle the toes.
4. **Step into the next lunge.** Re-engage at slower cadence.

Total stop time: 10-15 seconds. Compare to dropping the bag (5-burpee penalty + re-set + losing the carry position) which costs 60-90 seconds.

If you can do the whole 100 m without stopping, do that. If you must stop, the 4-step recovery is the cheapest way.

## Pacing: the wall ball test

The sandbag lunges sit before the wall ball. If you lunge too hard, your legs are toast for the 75 unbroken wall balls. If you lunge too easy, the wall ball is easier but you've lost 60 seconds on the lunge station.

The right pacing target: finish the lunge breathing hard but not gasping. Heart rate elevated but not at maximum. Legs heavy but able to squat.

A useful self-test in training: after a hard 100 m lunge in your wall ball preparation, can you immediately do 20 unbroken wall balls without resting? If yes, your lunge pacing is correct. If no, slow the lunge.

## Training the sandbag lunge

Two specific weekly sessions in the 6 weeks before race day:

**Session 1: distance work.** 4 × 100 m at race-bag weight, 2 min rest between. Unbroken. Builds the muscular endurance.

**Session 2: combo work.** 50 m sandbag lunge + 25 wall balls + 50 m sandbag lunge + 25 wall balls + 50 m sandbag lunge + 25 wall balls. One round. Builds the transition that decides race day.

That's it. More volume than this and the recovery starts hurting other sessions. Less and you arrive on race day with intact technique but no muscular endurance.

## The grip is overlooked

The sandbag lunge looks like a leg exercise but it's also a grip + core challenge. The bag rests on your shoulders but your hands hold it in place. If your hands slip, the bag rolls forward and you have to re-set or drop.

In training, never use lifting straps for sandbag work. The race-day grip comes from training grip.

## The honest summary

The sandbag lunge is the second most race-deciding station after the wall ball. Pacing matters more than strength. Technique matters more than power. The athletes who finish the lunge fast and arrive at the wall ball fresh are the ones who:

- Set up the bag correctly behind the neck
- Hold steady cadence (one lunge per 2-3 seconds)
- Breathe in a pattern, not gasps
- Don't go all-out; save for the wall ball

Train it weekly. Train it in a tired state. Race it at 75-80% effort. The minutes you save on the lunge by going hard, you lose on the wall ball by twice as much.
