CrossFitters convert to Hyrox better than almost any other fitness background. The strength, the work capacity, the mental tolerance for high-intensity work, all transfers. The conversion is 6-8 weeks of tweaks, not a full rebuild.

This is what to keep, what to add, and what to expect on race day.

## What transfers directly

**1. Strength.** A CrossFitter who can deadlift 1.5x body weight is strong enough for Hyrox's heaviest station (sled push at 152 kg for open men, equivalent to a moderate-load drag). The sled is not a strength limiter for CrossFitters.

**2. Metcon engine.** The ability to work hard for 10-30 minutes is exactly what Hyrox stations require. Wall ball at 100 reps unbroken? CrossFitters laugh at this. Burpee broad jump for 80 m? Familiar territory.

**3. Movement skill.** Wall ball, push press, lunges, broad jumps are all standard CrossFit movements. The technique is already in your nervous system.

**4. Pacing skill (intra-set).** CrossFitters know how to pace a 21-15-9, how to break a set of 100 KB swings, how to plan reps when fatigue spikes.

**5. Suffering tolerance.** Probably the biggest transfer. CrossFitters know how it feels to hold pace when everything hurts.

## What you'll find easy

Most stations:
- **Sled push:** Strong CrossFitters move it in 90-120 seconds. Not a limiter.
- **Sled pull:** Familiar movement pattern. 90-150 seconds.
- **Burpee broad jump:** A few practice runs and the pattern clicks.
- **Wall ball:** Familiar movement, 100 reps at 6 kg is mid-tier CrossFit territory.
- **Farmer's carry:** Carries are in many CrossFit programmes.
- **Sandbag lunges:** Lunges are familiar; the loaded version is just a thicker grip on the shoulder.
- **Rower:** Half of CrossFit's metcons involve the rower.

That's 7 of 8 stations a CrossFitter can do without specific training.

## What you'll find harder

**1. Sustained running.** This is the conversion's biggest gap. CrossFit metcons rarely involve more than 800 m of continuous running. Hyrox is 8 km. The aerobic system needs different training.

**2. Race-pace pacing across 90 minutes.** CrossFit pacing is for 10-25 minute efforts. Hyrox demands a different pacing skill: 90 minutes of consistent, sub-maximal output.

**3. Ski erg endurance.** CrossFit uses the ski erg occasionally but rarely for 1,000 m race-pace efforts. The shoulder + lat endurance for a 4-minute ski erg is specific.

## The 6-8 week conversion

**Weeks 1-2: aerobic base addition.**
- Keep 3 CrossFit classes per week.
- Add 3 easy aerobic runs (40-60 min each, conversational pace).
- Goal: comfortably run 60 minutes at easy pace by end of week 2.

**Weeks 3-4: race-pace running.**
- 2 CrossFit classes.
- 2 easy runs.
- 1 interval session: 5 × 1 km at goal race pace.
- 1 long aerobic (75-90 min).

**Weeks 5-6: race-specific work.**
- 1 CrossFit class.
- 1 easy run.
- 1 race-pace interval session.
- 1 station-specific session (sled + ski erg + wall ball at race weight).
- 1 race simulation (4 stations + 4 × 1 km runs).
- 1 long aerobic.

**Weeks 7-8: peak + taper.**
- 1 CrossFit class (light).
- Race simulation 2 weeks before race.
- Taper week 1: 60% volume.
- Taper week 2: 40% volume, maintain intensity.

## Sample conversion week (week 5)

| Day | Session |
|---|---|
| Mon | CrossFit class |
| Tue | Race-pace intervals: 5 × 1 km |
| Wed | Easy aerobic 45 min |
| Thu | Sled + ski erg + wall ball station session |
| Fri | Rest or mobility |
| Sat | Race simulation (4 stations + 4 km runs) |
| Sun | Long easy aerobic 90 min |

This is a 6-session week. Demanding. The intensity drop from CrossFit's 6-7 hard sessions to a more aerobic distribution is the right move.

## Pacing on race day

The single biggest race-day mistake CrossFitters make: treating Hyrox like a long metcon and going out at metcon pace.

A CrossFit metcon pace at 10-25 minutes works because the suffer window is short. Apply that pace to a 90-minute race and you blow up at station 5.

The right race-day pacing:
- **Stations 1-3:** 75-80% effort. Conservative. You're not racing yet.
- **Stations 4-5:** 80-85%. Settling into rhythm.
- **Stations 6-7:** 85% effort, technique-focused. This is where CrossFitters who paced wrong fall apart.
- **Station 8 + final run:** Empty the tank.

If your first 1 km run feels easy, you're pacing correctly.

## What to drop temporarily

For the 8 weeks of conversion:
- **Olympic lifting volume.** Cut max-effort snatches and cleans. You don't need them for Hyrox; they cost recovery.
- **Heavy 1RM testing.** Same reason.
- **Long gymnastic-skill sessions.** Save the muscle-up time for after race day.

You're not losing the skills; you're parking them for 8 weeks while you build aerobic capacity.

## What to keep

- **Olympic lift technique** (light weight). Maintains the pattern.
- **Push press, front squat** (moderate weight). Maintains the strength.
- **Box jumps, double-unders.** Useful for warm-ups and aerobic conditioning.

## The race-day expectation

A CrossFitter at moderate fitness (5+ years training) converting to Hyrox typically finishes:

- **First race:** 80-95 minutes. Limited by pacing inexperience, not fitness.
- **Second race (6 months later):** 75-85 minutes. Pacing learned.
- **Third race (12 months later):** Whatever your aerobic ceiling allows.

The fastest converters are CrossFitters who already run for fun. Hybrid athletes (CrossFit + running) often finish their first Hyrox sub-80.

## The honest summary

CrossFit gives you 70% of Hyrox fitness on day one. The 30% you need to add is aerobic endurance, race-pace pacing, and 1-2 station-specific sessions per week.

Don't drop CrossFit entirely. Reduce to 1-2 sessions per week during the build. Add running. Add sled + ski erg specifically. Race conservative, finish strong.

Most CrossFitters who convert end up adding Hyrox to their year rather than replacing CrossFit. Two complementary sports, six races between them, one solid year of training.
