The burpee broad jump is the station that decides the back half of your Hyrox race. Not because it's the hardest, the sled push is harder; the wall balls are longer. It's decisive because by the time you reach it, you've already spent four kilometres of running and two sled stations of effort, and the burpee broad jump has no rhythm to coast on. Each rep is a full reset: chest down, jump up, jump forward, stand. Twenty metres of that, with your heart rate already in the high 170s.

This piece covers the technique, the breathing pattern, the scaling options, and the most common pacing mistake that turns a 2:30 station into a 5-minute disaster.

## What the burpee broad jump actually demands

Twenty metres. One rep covers roughly 80-110 cm of forward progress for most athletes. Strong jumpers cover 130 cm. That's 18-30 reps per athlete, but the variability is wide, your standing broad jump determines it, and most people have never trained it specifically.

Three demands stack:

1. **Aerobic endurance.** Each rep raises your heart rate; you do not get to coast between them. The standing component is your only recovery and it lasts about half a second.
2. **Hip extension power.** The jump is a horizontal leap from a near-squat. If your jump is short, you do more reps. More reps means more burpees. More burpees means more time.
3. **Postural control under fatigue.** The chest-to-floor portion of the burpee can degrade into a sloppy crawl as fatigue builds. Slow, ugly burpees are dangerous and waste energy.

## Body position: tight, neutral, hinged

Three positions, in order of importance:

**1. Tight midline through the descent.** When you drop to the floor, your body should land as a controlled unit. Not feet first, then hips, then chest. All at once, with your core braced. Slack midlines waste energy and risk a tweaked back when fatigue hits in the second half.

**2. Neutral neck.** Look at the floor about 30 cm ahead of your hands as you go down. Looking forward (chin up) makes the burpee feel harder by extending the cervical spine. Look at the floor.

**3. Hinge, don't squat.** The transition from burpee to broad jump is a hinge, push the floor away, drive your hips forward, and load the posterior chain for the jump. If you stand straight up and then try to broad jump, you've already lost the elastic energy that makes the leap efficient.

## The breathing pattern that holds together

Most athletes default to one of two breathing patterns and both are bad:

**Bad pattern A. Breath-holding.** You hold the breath through the burpee, gasp on the jump, then hold again. Five reps in and you're light-headed. Your VO2 collapses.

**Bad pattern B. Hyperventilation.** You panic-breathe at every transition. Twenty-five short breaths per minute. Net oxygen exchange is poor because tidal volume is too low.

**What works, the three-count.**

- Breath out on the descent (chest to floor).
- Breath in on the stand-up.
- Breath out on the jump.

It's a controlled, slow, audible exhale on impact and on push-off. Sounds like a controlled grunt. Practise it in training, by race day, this should be automatic.

## Foot positioning: shoulder-width to slightly wider

The broad jump is biomechanically a horizontal squat-jump. Your feet want to be roughly shoulder-width apart, or slightly wider if you have hip mobility constraints. Wider feet means less hamstring/glute drive, narrower feet means an unstable platform.

After the jump, land with bent knees absorbing the impact. Do not lock out on the landing, that's a knee injury waiting to happen, especially when fatigued.

## The arm swing

The arm swing is free distance. Athletes who don't use their arms lose 15-20 cm per jump. Over 20 metres that's two to three extra reps, and at 8-12 seconds per rep that's 16-36 extra seconds of station time.

How to use the arms:

1. Hands push off the floor at the end of the burpee.
2. Swing them back behind you as you stand and hinge.
3. Swing them forward and up as you jump.

The arm swing initiates the jump. It is not an afterthought. Practise it in training without the burpee, just standing broad jumps with a rhythmic arm swing, until it feels automatic.

## Scaling: when and how

Two legitimate scaling decisions:

**Scaling for first-time finishers.** If you can complete a clean burpee broad jump but worry about the volume, you can break it up. The race standards do not require unbroken sets. You can take a knee, take a breath, take five steps if you need to. The clock is running, so you want fewer breaks rather than more, but a planned ten-second break at the halfway mark is better than a 60-second collapse at metre 18.

**Scaling for injuries.** If a wrist injury makes the burpee dangerous, you can substitute a no-press burpee (chest to floor, hands off the floor on the way up, knees and hips do the work). This is slower per rep but safer. If a knee injury makes the broad jump dangerous, step out of the jump and do a clean stride forward instead, note that this is a personal scaling decision; competitive Hyrox standards require the broad jump portion to be a true two-foot take-off and landing.

<Callout tone="warn" title="The first-kilometre trap">
The biggest pacing mistake in Hyrox isn't on the burpee broad jump itself. It's on the first kilometre of running. If you ran the opening km at 4:00 pace because you felt fresh, you'll do the burpee broad jump at twice the cardio cost. Run the opening km 10-15 seconds slower than your target average. Your burpee broad jump split will reward you.
</Callout>

## Drills that transfer to race day

Three drills, in order of impact:

**1. Race-pace burpee broad jump intervals.** Once a week during the build phase. Three rounds of 10 reps with 90-second rest. The point isn't volume, it's practising the rhythm and breathing under fatigue.

**2. Maximal standing broad jumps.** Twice a week, six sets of three, full rest between sets. Builds the absolute jump distance. Most athletes can add 15-25 cm to their standing broad jump over a 12-week build with consistent training. That alone shaves 30-45 seconds off the station.

**3. Burpee-to-tall-jump.** Replaces the broad jump with a vertical jump for variety. Same burpee mechanics, different output. Useful for athletes who train at home with limited horizontal space.

## What good looks like

A good Hyrox burpee broad jump at age-group level:

- Time: 2:30 to 3:30.
- Reps: 20-25.
- Heart rate: peaks at 90-95% of max, sustained.
- Breathing: audible, rhythmic, three-count pattern.
- Body position: flat midline on the floor, hinged hips on the stand, full arm swing on the jump.

If your station time is 4:30+, the fix is almost never "more burpees in training". The fix is improving jump distance (more strength work, more standing broad jumps) and fixing the breathing pattern. Both compound fast.

## To sum up

The burpee broad jump rewards three things: a controlled breathing rhythm, a long broad jump, and honest pacing on the kilometres before it. Most first-timers lose two minutes here for free, because nobody taught them the rhythm. The technique is learnable in two weeks. The fitness takes the full twelve.
